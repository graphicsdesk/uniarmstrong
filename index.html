As Shafik’s legacy looms, Armstrong faces a tumultuous road ahead

By Emily Forgash, Joseph Zuloaga. Edited by Isabella Ramírez, Sarah Huddleston, Shea Vance, Yvin Shin, Ella Ahner, Neena Dzur, and Sophia Lee

August 17, 2024

Judy Goldstein / Deputy Photo Editor

The presidency of a University squarely in the center of the tensions roiling college campuses since Oct. 7, 2023, seems far from a dream job.

But Katrina Armstrong, the Columbia University Irving Medical Center’s chief executive officer and Columbia’s new interim president, has signed on to succeed Minouche Shafik, whose tenure was embroiled by student protests, congressional scrutiny, and backlash over administrative decision-making.

Although her career in leadership at universities and hospitals spans decades, Armstrong, the first woman to lead the medical center and Vagelos College of Physicians and Surgeons, is relatively new to the University. With only two years at Columbia under her belt, Armstrong brings a blank slate and a legacy marked by distinguished research and leadership at a highly-ranked medical school.

The most significant controversy she has faced in her tenure was criticism over the University and medical center’s handling of the case involving former medical center gynecologist Robert Hadden, who was convicted on federal charges of sexually abusing patients for two decades. It was a serious issue passed down to her by previous leadership, much like those she has inherited from Shafik.

Even then, Armstrong led the medical center as it moved to address the harm inflicted on Hadden’s patients and establish preventive measures against future abuse.

[Read more: What to know about Katrina Armstrong, Columbia’s new interim president]

Armstrong wrote in her first email to the Columbia community as interim president on Wednesday that she is “acutely aware of the trials the University has faced over the past year.” As an administrator, she has delivered remarks on antisemitism, Oct. 7, and the humanitarian crisis in Gaza.

On Friday, she issued another statement urging Columbia to “recalibrate as a community” while “bearing in mind the challenges of the past year and those to come, but not allowing them to define us.”

“We must take care to bridge divides,” she wrote.

What remains to be seen, however, is how Armstrong will rebuild a fractured University with a new vision for Columbia while carrying the baggage of Shafik’s presidency—and, how long she will be tasked with steering it.

The interim presidency

Armstrong is the first person in Columbia’s history to hold the title of interim president, although there have been two acting presidents, Frank D. Fackenthal from 1945 to 1948 and Andrew W. Cordier from 1968 to 1969, and one president pro tempore, Benjamin Moore from 1775 to 1776.

All these presidents stepped in during or directly after major wars: Moore amid the American Revolutionary War, Fackenthal after World War II, and Cordier during the Vietnam War. Cordier stepped in after Grayson Kirk resigned following the upheaval of the 1968 protests.

While historically rare for Columbia, Armstrong’s appointment follows a recent trend among the Ivy League, which has seen three other interim presidents in the past year.

University of Pennsylvania’s Larry Jameson and Harvard University’s Alan Garber both became interim presidents after the universities’ respective leaders, Liz Magill and Claudine Gay, stepped down after struggling to respond to intense questioning about antisemitism at a Dec. 5, 2023, congressional hearing. Months later, Cornell University tapped Michael Kotlikoff as its interim president following the retirement of Martha Pollack.

Armstrong joins her fellow interim presidents in hailing from her institution’s medical school, although she is the only woman among her interim peers. Prior to the past months of leadership shakeup, women leaders oversaw six out of eight Ivy League universities. Now, that number is down to four.

Earlier this summer, the University of Pennsylvania announced Jameson would remain as interim president through at least 2026, or until a successor is found. At Harvard, Garber became the university’s 31st president two weeks ago and will hold the position until the end of the 2026-27 academic year. Neither university has officially launched its presidential search.

Similarly, Columbia has yet to release any information regarding a search for Shafik’s permanent replacement. Columbia’s last presidential search took roughly seven months from the day the board of trustees launched the presidential search committee in June 2022 to the day the University announced Shafik as the 20th president in January 2023. Former University President Lee Bollinger had said he was stepping down in April 2022.

New community initiatives

As Armstrong takes the helm, she will be tasked with overseeing Shafik’s ongoing community initiatives designed to tackle concerns over academic freedom, staff needs, and Columbia’s long-existing protest rules.

The list of initiatives, which Shafik outlined in a July 24 email to the Columbia community, placed emphasis on surveying opinion from the University’s faculty, staff, and student constituencies, although in broad terms and with little detail on concrete steps to help bridge divides. Shafik wrote that Chief Operating Officer Cas Holloway is working to “build our capacity to better understand the concerns and feelings of our workforce,” and Executive Vice President for University Life Dennis Mitchell has met with students over the summer to solicit feedback.

In the email, Shafik also wrote that University Provost Angela Olinto has been “working with each of our deans to set up faculty working groups focused on sustaining academic freedom and free speech in a community free of discrimination and bias.” She added that these bodies will be “essential” in educating future leaders—a sentiment Armstrong echoed in her Wednesday email to the community.

“You are the ultimate keepers of the institution’s values and the stewards of its long and proud history,” Armstrong wrote of the University’s faculty. “The habit of critical thinking and humility that gives birth to tolerance of contrary points of view is the most essential lesson taught in Columbia’s classrooms.”

Another one of Shafik’s initiatives makes a nod to the concerns raised by student protesters, such as breakdowns in “good faith engagement.” She wrote that the University asked two faculty from the Negotiation and Conflict Resolution Program at the School of Professional Studies to work with affected students toward mediated outcomes, which Armstrong—without the blame for April’s two police sweeps—will be key in upholding.

Negotiations between Columbia’s administration and protesters in the “Gaza Solidarity Encampment” collapsed in April after students declined the University’s offers and Shafik announced that Columbia “will not divest from Israel.” Pro-Palestinian students have indicated they will continue in the fall to demand divestment from all companies with ties to Israel and cancellation of the opening of the Tel Aviv Global Center, leaving Armstrong with the challenging task of balancing clashing concerns over the handling of future protests.

An era of administrative turnover

Shafik’s departure and Armstrong’s appointment mark Columbia’s most stunning administrative change within the past year, but they followed a semester of administrative turnover that extended across the University.

In January, Columbia hired Holloway, the University’s first-ever chief operating officer and a now key administrative figure in the University’s attempts to discipline students who it identifies as participating in unauthorized demonstrations or events.

Holloway announced in March the University’s investigation into a “Resistance 101” event, which featured a speaker allegedly part of a group the United States has designated as a terrorist organization. The University suspended and evicted multiple students for their alleged involvement in the event, and, that same day, Holloway began contacting dozens of student leaders from organizations signed onto Columbia University Apartheid Divest ahead of a planned “All Out for Al-Shifa Protest.”

Over 100 demonstrators gathered at the Sundial on April 4 to protest Israel’s military’s siege and takeover of Al-Shifa Hospital in Gaza. Photo by Gabriella Gregor-Splaver / Deputy Photo Editor

Prior to Holloway, former Senior Executive Vice President Gerald Rosberg played the most public role in the University’s handling of pro-Palestinian protests. Rosberg served as the chair of the Special Committee on Public Safety, which met regularly throughout the fall semester and whose membership was composed of eight top administrators.

He signed the November letter announcing the suspensions of the Columbia chapters of Students for Justice in Palestine and Jewish Voice for Peace and confirmed that the University unilaterally revised its events policies just 17 days before they were cited in the groups’ suspensions. Rosberg retired from his post on July 1.

The University also found its newest provost in Angela Olinto, who assumed the position on April 1.

Less than two weeks before Shafik stepped down, three Columbia College deans resigned following their involvement in a controversy surrounding texts the administrators sent during a panel on Jewish student life on May 31. The texts were leaked to the Washington Free Beacon, and the House Committee on Education and the Workforce demanded Columbia hand over the full exchange, which the committee published.

Former Vice Dean and Chief Administrative Officer Susan Chang-Kim, former Dean of Undergraduate Student Life Cristen Kromm, and former Associate Dean for Student and Family Support Matthew Patashnick were first placed on leave before resigning. Columbia College Dean Josef Sorett participated in the exchange, though to a lesser extent, and has remained in his role.

Discipline and the University Senate

Armstrong must also tackle decisions on how disciplinary processes against protesters will move forward. Students undergoing the processes have declared them as overly punitive and unfair, but Congress says University administrators are not doing enough to crack down on protest and protect Jewish students.

Columbia suspended dozens of students for their alleged involvement in the “Gaza Solidarity Encampment” and continues to pursue disciplinary action against those it identified as participating in the Hamilton Hall occupation. Those cases are now under the jurisdiction of the University Judicial Board, a branch of the University Senate.

Police swept Hamilton on April 30, arresting 109 protesters in and around the building. Photo by Heather Chen / Sports Editor

As interim president, Armstrong will serve as the presiding officer of the University Senate and sit on its executive committee. The University Senate is a governing body composed of members of Columbia’s seven constituencies: faculty, administrative staff, students, administrators, librarians, research officers, and alumni.

During Shafik’s presidency, she attended only a few of the senate’s monthly plenary sessions, raising concerns from senators. The next senate plenary is scheduled for Friday, Aug. 23, although it remains unclear whether Armstrong will attend.

The University Senate’s Rules of University Conduct were brushed aside for much of the spring semester as Columbia introduced its interim policy for safe demonstrations in February, which generated immediate backlash from members of the University Senate. The policy is subject to review and revision, though no changes have been made public despite the policy being in place for almost six months.

The Rules of University Conduct—which are codified in the University Statutes and have existed since the 1968 protests—are also undergoing their own review and revision through the University Senate.

Armstrong will also have to contend with Columbia’s proposal to add “peace officers” to Columbia’s security personnel, as reported by the Wall Street Journal. Columbia currently has around 290 security personnel, according to the Journal. While sources told the Journal the proposal is still in its early stages, they said the officers could be granted the authority to arrest students.

A University spokesperson wrote in a statement to Spectator that Columbia is undergoing a process of internal and external consultations with other universities to prepare “for a range of scenarios that may arise.” The spokesperson did not confirm nor deny the basis of the Journal’s report.

“We are looking at various ways to supplement our public safety capabilities,” the spokesperson wrote. “We seek to strengthen the department’s skills and training in de-escalation techniques, expanding the department’s ability to manage a range of incident[s] while taking into account the fact Columbia does not have its own police force, as many peer institutions have, and potentially reducing our reliance on the NYPD.”

Other issues confronting Armstrong

Campus tensions over the war in Gaza have at times highlighted, and at other times overshadowed, a myriad of issues that have long been central focuses for the University.

One such issue is Columbia’s endowment as it begins to recover from a $1 billion slump after the height of the COVID-19 pandemic. The Columbia Investment Management Company manages the majority of the University’s assets, which outside of some information on Columbia’s 990 forms, are largely unknown to the public.

The Advisory Committee on Socially Responsible Investing rejected in February a proposal from Columbia University Apartheid Divest, titled “Divestment from Israeli Apartheid, Occupation, and Genocide,” on the basis of failing to meet the criteria for consideration of divestment. The proposal cited investments in companies such as Microsoft, Alphabet Inc., Amazon, and an exchange-traded fund managed by BlackRock—all of which have operations in Israel.

Shafik’s administration had offered to protesters that Columbia develop an “expedited timeline” for divestment proposals submitted to the committee for review and establish a process by which students can access a list of Columbia’s direct investment holdings, although neither offer moved forward when negotiations fell through the night of April 23.

Should Armstrong enter negotiations again with protesters, she will have to weigh the pros and cons of previous offers and proposals, both from students and the University.

The second encampment lasted a total of 13 days. Photo by Judy Goldstein / Deputy Photo Editor

In every decision she makes, Armstrong will have to weigh the donor response. Many donors, such as Robert Kraft, CC ’63, have pulled their funding from Columbia as a show of disagreement with the way the administration handled the crisis on campus. Columbia also indefinitely postponed last year’s Giving Day—the annual 24-hour online fundraising initiative that usually takes place in October—for the first time in the event’s 12-year history.

Other Ivy League schools have also seen their donors retract funding as a result of ongoing campus activism and administrative decision-making. Harvard’s gift officers became increasingly worried that longtime donors would stop giving money over concerns regarding antisemitism on campus, the Harvard Crimson reported in November, and the Daily Pennsylvanian reported that dozens of alumni pulled their funding around the same time.

A contentious aspect of Bollinger’s legacy, Columbia’s Manhattanville campus expansion, became much less of a focus of criticism for Shafik despite construction continuing into its second phase, which is set to conclude in 2030. Still, in June, West Harlem leaders and students gathered for a “Juneteenth Rally for Housing Justice” in support of a State Senate bill that would immediately halt Columbia’s expansion into Harlem. One of the rally’s co-sponsors was SJP.

The bill is still in the early stages and may see further developments throughout Armstrong’s tenure.

Another issue left unresolved by Bollinger and Shafik is the potential restructuring and expansion of the Faculty of Arts and Sciences, the budgetary unit encompassing five schools: Columbia College, the School of General Studies, the School of Professional Studies, the Graduate School of the Arts and Sciences, and the School of the Arts.

Restructuring efforts, which Bollinger set out to explore by creating a task force in September 2021, attempted to deal with questions about the administration of the Arts and Sciences. Much of the debate surrounded the budgetary relationship between Columbia College and FAS, with concerns over the discrepancy in fundraising between Columbia College and the Arts and Sciences as a whole.

As part of an effort to bring in more funding, FAS launched an inquiry in 2021 into potentially increasing the number of undergraduate students. The plan was postponed in September 2022 until fall or spring 2025 to coincide with the completion of renovations to Uris Hall.

However, as a result of the Arts and Sciences’ continued funding issues, the University has not begun construction on Uris and will likely see an extension to the project’s timeline. Armstrong may have to help decide the future of the project as it lays stagnant.

FAS passed a vote of no confidence in Shafik that was announced on May 16, with 65 percent of participating faculty members voting in favor. The vote was brought to the faculty by the Columbia of the American Association of University Professors, which expressed concerns about the state of shared governance and academic freedom at Columbia as well as the disciplinary proceedings mounted this past semester.

Now, Armstrong will have to face the outspoken criticisms from the past academic year head-on, and assuming her tenure extends to 2025 or beyond, possibly decide the future of expansion, restructuring, and renovations to Uris.

A fresh start

Shafik’s resignation largely came as a surprise to the community, especially after she weathered the spring semester without backing out of her role during a comparably quiet summer. But, her timing dodged the potential chaos of undergoing a presidential change in the middle of an academic year, which occurred at Harvard and the University of Pennsylvania.

As Armstrong inherits a mountain of pressing issues left by Shafik concerning the University’s standing in higher education and the nation at large, the community will watch to see how she makes her mark as a new leader in a time when all eyes are on Columbia.
